Existence and Uniqueness of Square Roots

This lecture gives a complete, rigorous proof that every non-negative real number has a (unique) non-negative square root. The proof is a beautiful application of the Least Upper Bound (LUB) Property of $\mathbb{R}$ combined with the Archimedean Property. Before that try to understand few of following problems related to finding lub and glb of bounded sets.

Q1: Find lub and glb of $\{1/n \mid n\in \mathbb{N}\}$
The set is $S = \{1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, \dots\}$.
1. Least Upper Bound (lub): The largest element in the set is $1$ (when $n=1$). Since the maximum exists, the supremum (lub) is $1$. $$ \text{lub}(S) = 1 $$
2. Greatest Lower Bound (glb): All elements are positive, so $0$ is a lower bound. By the Archimedean Property, for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/n < \epsilon$. Thus, no number greater than $0$ can be a lower bound. $$ \text{glb}(S) = 0 $$
Q3: Find lub and glb of $\{a_n \mid a_1=1, a_2=2, a_{n+2}=\frac{a_n+a_{n+1}}{2}\}$
1. Analysis of Bounds: The sequence is the average of the previous two terms. This implies it is bounded by the minimum and maximum of the initial terms. Since $a_1=1$ and $a_2=2$, the sequence is bounded between $1$ and $2$.
2. Checking Extremes: Calculating the first few terms: $1, 2, 1.5, 1.75, 1.625, 1.6875, \dots$ The odd terms $1, 1.5, 1.625, \dots$ form an increasing subsequence starting at $1$. The even terms $2, 1.75, 1.6875, \dots$ form a decreasing subsequence starting at $2$. The value $1$ is actually achieved ($a_1$), so it is the minimum. The value $2$ is actually achieved ($a_2$), so it is the maximum.
3. lub and glb: Since the maximum and minimum exist in the set, they are equal to the lub and glb respectively. $$ \text{lub} = 2, \quad \text{glb} = 1 $$
Q7: Find lub and glb of $A = \left\{\frac{m+n}{mn} \;\middle|\; m,n \in \mathbb{N}\right\}$
1. Simplify the Expression: Before finding bounds, we simplify the algebraic expression defining the set. For any $m, n \in \mathbb{N}$: $$ \frac{m+n}{mn} = \frac{m}{mn} + \frac{n}{mn} = \frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m} $$ So, the set can be rewritten as $A = \left\{ \frac{1}{m} + \frac{1}{n} \;\middle|\; m,n \in \mathbb{N} \right\}$.
2. Least Upper Bound (lub): Since $m, n \ge 1$, the maximum possible value for $\frac{1}{m}$ is $1$ (which occurs when $m=1$). Similarly, the maximum possible value for $\frac{1}{n}$ is $1$ (when $n=1$).
Therefore, for any $m, n \in \mathbb{N}$: $$ \frac{1}{m} + \frac{1}{n} \le 1 + 1 = 2 $$ This upper bound is actually achieved when we choose $m=1$ and $n=1$: $$ \frac{1+1}{1 \cdot 1} = 2 \in A $$ Since the maximum element of the set exists and is $2$, the least upper bound is simply the maximum. $$ \text{lub}(A) = 2 $$
3. Greatest Lower Bound (glb): Since $m, n \in \mathbb{N}$, we know $\frac{1}{m} > 0$ and $\frac{1}{n} > 0$. Thus, every element in $A$ is strictly positive, meaning $0$ is a lower bound for $A$.

To show $0$ is the greatest lower bound, we must show that no number larger than $0$ can be a lower bound. Consider choosing $m = n$. The element becomes: $$ \frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{n} = \frac{2}{n} $$ By the Archimedean Property, for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > \frac{2}{\epsilon}$, which implies $\frac{2}{n} < \epsilon$.
Because we can find elements in $A$ arbitrarily close to $0$ (but strictly greater than $0$), no number $\epsilon > 0$ can be a lower bound. $$ \text{glb}(A) = 0 $$ *(Note: $0$ is not in $A$ because $m,n \in \mathbb{N}$ guarantees the sum is strictly positive, so $A$ has a glb but no minimum.)*

Step 1 — The Theorem

Theorem — Existence and Uniqueness of Square Roots
Informal statement: Every non-negative real number has a square root.

Precise statement: Let $\alpha\in\mathbb{R}$ with $\alpha\ge 0.$ Then there exists a unique $a\in\mathbb{R}$ with $a\ge 0$ such that $$a^2 = \alpha.$$ We write $a = \sqrt{\alpha}.$
Two things to prove: both existence ($a$ exists) and uniqueness ($a$ is the only such non-negative number). These are separate tasks and require separate arguments.
Why is this non-trivial?
In $\mathbb{Q}$ the theorem fails: $\alpha=2$ has no rational square root (proved by the classic irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$ argument). The theorem holds in $\mathbb{R}$ precisely because $\mathbb{R}$ is complete — it has the LUB Property. This proof is therefore a direct demonstration of the power of completeness.

Proof Roadmap

  • 1 Handle the trivial case $\alpha = 0$ separately.
  • 2 For $\alpha>0$, construct the set $E_\alpha = \{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x^2\le\alpha\}.$
  • 3 Show $E_\alpha$ is nonempty.
  • 4 Show $E_\alpha$ is bounded above.
  • 5 Apply the LUB Property: let $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha).$
  • 6 Show $a^2 = \alpha$ by ruling out $a^2 < \alpha$ and $a^2 > \alpha.$
  • 7 Prove uniqueness.

Step 2 — The Case $\alpha = 0$

  • ✓ Handle the trivial case $\alpha = 0.$
  • 2 Construct $E_\alpha.$
  • 3 $E_\alpha$ nonempty.
  • 4 $E_\alpha$ bounded above.
  • 5 Apply LUB Property.
  • 6 Show $a^2=\alpha.$
  • 7 Uniqueness.
Case $\alpha = 0$
Existence: Take $a = 0.$ Then $a\ge 0$ and $a^2 = 0 = \alpha.$ ✓

Uniqueness: Suppose $a\ge 0$ and $a^2 = 0.$ Since $a\ge 0$ and $a^2 = a\cdot a = 0,$ we must have $a = 0$ (a product of reals is zero only if at least one factor is zero).

So $a = 0$ is the unique non-negative square root of $0.$ ✓
From now on we assume $\alpha > 0$ and prove both existence and uniqueness in this case.

Step 3 — Constructing the Set $E_\alpha$

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • 2 Construct $E_\alpha.$ ← Now
  • 3 $E_\alpha$ nonempty.
  • 4 $E_\alpha$ bounded above.
  • 5 Apply LUB Property.
  • 6 Show $a^2=\alpha.$
  • 7 Uniqueness.

We now assume $\alpha > 0.$ The key idea is to encode the square root as the least upper bound of a carefully chosen set.

Definition of $E_\alpha$
$$E_\alpha \;=\; \{\,x\in\mathbb{R} \;\mid\; x^2 \le \alpha\,\}.$$ This is the set of all real numbers whose square does not exceed $\alpha.$
Strategy — Why this set?
If $\sqrt{\alpha}$ exists, then the elements of $E_\alpha$ are exactly those $x$ with $|x|\le\sqrt{\alpha},$ i.e., $E_\alpha = [-\sqrt{\alpha},\sqrt{\alpha}].$ So $\text{lub}(E_\alpha) = \sqrt{\alpha}.$

We cannot use $\sqrt{\alpha}$ directly (that is what we are trying to prove exists!), but we can use the LUB of $E_\alpha$ — and then prove it equals $\sqrt{\alpha}.$ This is the key circular-free construction.

We need to verify three things before we can apply the LUB Property:

  1. $E_\alpha$ is nonempty.
  2. $E_\alpha$ is bounded above.
  3. Then the LUB Property guarantees $\text{lub}(E_\alpha)$ exists in $\mathbb{R}.$

Step 4 — $E_\alpha$ is Nonempty

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • ✓ $E_\alpha$ constructed.
  • 3 $E_\alpha$ nonempty. ← Now
  • 4 $E_\alpha$ bounded above.
  • 5 Apply LUB Property.
  • 6 Show $a^2=\alpha.$
  • 7 Uniqueness.
Proof — $E_\alpha$ is nonempty. Observation 1: $0^2 = 0 \le \alpha,$ so $0\in E_\alpha.$ Hence $E_\alpha\ne\emptyset.$ ✓

Observation 2 (stronger): $E_\alpha$ actually contains a strictly positive element — a fact we will need later.

Since $\alpha > 0,$ the Archimedean Property gives $n\in\mathbb{N}$ with $\dfrac{1}{n} < \alpha.$ Then: $$\frac{1}{n^2} \;\le\; \frac{1}{n} \;<\; \alpha,$$ so $\left(\dfrac{1}{n}\right)^2 = \dfrac{1}{n^2} < \alpha,$ hence $\dfrac{1}{n}\in E_\alpha.$ Since $\dfrac{1}{n} > 0,$ we have found a positive element of $E_\alpha.$ ✓ $\blacksquare$
Why the stronger observation matters
Knowing $0\in E_\alpha$ alone would give $\text{lub}(E_\alpha)\ge 0.$ But knowing $\tfrac{1}{n}\in E_\alpha$ for some $n>0$ gives $\text{lub}(E_\alpha)\ge\tfrac{1}{n}>0,$ so $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha)>0.$ This strict positivity is needed in Step 6 when we divide by $a.$

Step 5 — $E_\alpha$ is Bounded Above & the LUB Exists

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • ✓ $E_\alpha$ constructed.
  • ✓ $E_\alpha$ nonempty.
  • 4 $E_\alpha$ bounded above. ← Now
  • 5 Apply LUB Property. ← Now
  • 6 Show $a^2=\alpha.$
  • 7 Uniqueness.
Proof — $E_\alpha$ is bounded above. By the Archimedean Property, there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ such that $N > \alpha.$

Since $N\in\mathbb{N}$ and $N\ge 1,$ we have $N^2 \ge N > \alpha.$

Claim: $N$ is an upper bound for $E_\alpha.$

Proof of claim (by contradiction): Suppose $N$ is not an upper bound. Then there exists $x\in E_\alpha$ with $x > N.$ Since $x > N > 0,$ squaring preserves the inequality: $$x^2 \;>\; N^2 \;\ge\; N \;>\; \alpha.$$ But $x\in E_\alpha$ means $x^2\le\alpha$ — a contradiction. Hence $N$ is an upper bound for $E_\alpha.$ ✓ $\blacksquare$
Applying the LUB Property
$E_\alpha$ is nonempty (Step 4) and bounded above (Step 5). By the Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ (LUB Property): $$a \;:=\; \text{lub}(E_\alpha) \;\in\; \mathbb{R} \quad\text{exists.}$$ Since $E_\alpha$ contains the positive element $\tfrac{1}{n}$ (Step 4), we have $a \ge \tfrac{1}{n} > 0,$ so $a > 0.$
Goal from here: We must show $a^2 = \alpha.$ We do this by the Law of Trichotomy: prove $a^2 < \alpha$ is impossible, and $a^2 > \alpha$ is impossible, so $a^2 = \alpha$ is the only remaining option.

Step 6 — Ruling Out $a^2 < \alpha$

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • ✓ $E_\alpha$ nonempty & bounded above.
  • ✓ $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha)$ exists, $a > 0.$
  • 6a Show $a^2 < \alpha$ is impossible. ← Now
  • 6b Show $a^2 > \alpha$ is impossible.
  • 7 Uniqueness.
Claim: $a^2 < \alpha$ is impossible
If $a^2 < \alpha,$ we will find $b\in E_\alpha$ with $b > a,$ contradicting $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha).$
✏ Rough Work — How to find $b$?
We try $b = a + \dfrac{1}{n}$ for some $n\in\mathbb{N}$ chosen large enough. We need $b\in E_\alpha,$ i.e., $b^2\le\alpha.$

Expand: $$b^2 = \left(a+\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 = a^2 + \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n^2}.$$ The term $\dfrac{1}{n^2}$ is harder to control, but note $\dfrac{1}{n^2}\le\dfrac{1}{n}$ for $n\ge 1,$ so: $$b^2 \;\le\; a^2 + \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n} = a^2 + \frac{2a+1}{n}.$$ If we can arrange $a^2 + \dfrac{2a+1}{n} \le\alpha,$ then $b^2\le\alpha.$ This requires: $$\frac{2a+1}{n} \;\le\; \alpha - a^2 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad n \;\ge\; \frac{2a+1}{\alpha - a^2}.$$ Since $\alpha - a^2 > 0$ (our assumption), $\dfrac{2a+1}{\alpha-a^2}$ is a finite positive number. The Archimedean Property guarantees such an $n\in\mathbb{N}$ exists. ✓
Actual Proof — $a^2 < \alpha$ leads to contradiction. Suppose $a^2 < \alpha,$ so $\alpha - a^2 > 0.$

Since $2a+1 > 0$ and $\alpha-a^2 > 0,$ by the Archimedean Property there exists $n\in\mathbb{N}$ such that: $$n(\alpha - a^2) \;>\; 2a+1,$$ equivalently $\dfrac{2a+1}{n} < \alpha - a^2.$

Set $b = a + \dfrac{1}{n}.$ Then $b > a$ and: $$b^2 = \left(a+\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 = a^2 + \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n^2} \;\le\; a^2 + \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n} = a^2 + \frac{2a+1}{n} \;<\; a^2 + (\alpha - a^2) = \alpha.$$ Therefore $b^2 < \alpha,$ so $b\in E_\alpha.$

But $b = a + \tfrac{1}{n} > a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha),$ which is a contradiction (no element of $E_\alpha$ can exceed the least upper bound).

Hence $a^2 < \alpha$ is impossible. $\blacksquare$
Why we dropped $1/n^2$
The bound $\dfrac{1}{n^2}\le\dfrac{1}{n}$ (valid for $n\ge 1$) lets us replace the quadratic term $\dfrac{1}{n^2}$ by the linear term $\dfrac{1}{n},$ making the algebra clean. The loss is harmless: we only need an upper bound on $b^2,$ and the cruder bound still gives us $b^2 <\alpha.$

Step 7 — Ruling Out $a^2 > \alpha$

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • ✓ $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha)$ exists, $a > 0.$
  • ✓ $a^2 < \alpha$ ruled out.
  • 6b Show $a^2 > \alpha$ is impossible. ← Now
  • 7 Uniqueness.
Claim: $a^2 > \alpha$ is impossible
If $a^2 > \alpha,$ we will find $c < a$ that is already an upper bound for $E_\alpha,$ contradicting $a$ being the least upper bound.
✏ Rough Work — How to find a smaller upper bound?
We try $c = a - \dfrac{1}{n}$ for some $n\in\mathbb{N}.$ We need $c > 0$ (so it is a valid upper bound candidate) and $c^2 > \alpha$ (so every $x$ with $x^2\le\alpha$ satisfies $x\le c < a$).

Expand: $$c^2 = \left(a-\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 = a^2 - \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n^2} \;\ge\; a^2 - \frac{2a}{n}.$$ We want $a^2 - \dfrac{2a}{n} > \alpha,$ i.e., $\dfrac{2a}{n} < a^2-\alpha,$ i.e., $$n \;>\; \frac{2a}{a^2-\alpha}.$$ Since $a^2-\alpha > 0$ (our assumption) and $2a > 0,$ the Archimedean Property gives such an $n.$ We also need $n > \dfrac{1}{a}$ so that $c = a - \tfrac{1}{n} > 0.$ Both conditions can be met simultaneously. ✓
Actual Proof — $a^2 > \alpha$ leads to contradiction. Suppose $a^2 > \alpha,$ so $a^2 - \alpha > 0.$

Since $2a > 0$ and $a^2-\alpha > 0,$ by the Archimedean Property there exists $n\in\mathbb{N}$ such that simultaneously: $$n \;>\; \frac{2a}{a^2-\alpha} \qquad\text{and}\qquad n \;>\; \frac{1}{a}.$$ (We can take $n$ larger than both finite quantities.)

The first condition gives $\dfrac{2a}{n} < a^2-\alpha.$
The second condition gives $\dfrac{1}{n} < a,$ so $c:=a-\dfrac{1}{n} > 0.$

Now estimate $c^2$ from below: $$c^2 = \left(a-\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 = a^2 - \frac{2a}{n} + \frac{1}{n^2} \;\ge\; a^2 - \frac{2a}{n} \;>\; a^2 - (a^2-\alpha) = \alpha.$$ So $c^2 > \alpha.$

We claim $c$ is an upper bound for $E_\alpha.$ Let $x\in E_\alpha,$ so $x^2\le\alpha < c^2.$ Since $c > 0,$ taking square roots (which preserve order for non-negatives): $x \le |x| \le c.$ (More carefully: if $x > c > 0$ then $x^2 > c^2 > \alpha,$ contradicting $x\in E_\alpha.$) Hence $x\le c$ for all $x\in E_\alpha,$ so $c$ is an upper bound for $E_\alpha.$

But $c = a - \dfrac{1}{n} < a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha),$ which means $c$ is an upper bound strictly less than the least upper bound — a contradiction.

Hence $a^2 > \alpha$ is impossible. $\blacksquare$

Step 8 — Conclusion: Existence & Uniqueness

  • ✓ $\alpha = 0$ handled.
  • ✓ $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha)$ exists, $a > 0.$
  • ✓ $a^2 < \alpha$ ruled out.
  • ✓ $a^2 > \alpha$ ruled out.
  • 7 Conclude $a^2=\alpha$ and prove uniqueness. ← Now
Existence — $a^2 = \alpha$
We have shown $a^2 < \alpha$ is false and $a^2 > \alpha$ is false. By the Law of Trichotomy, exactly one of $a^2 < \alpha,\; a^2 = \alpha,\; a^2 > \alpha$ holds. The only remaining possibility is $$a^2 = \alpha.$$ Since $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha) > 0,$ we have $a\ge 0$ and $a^2=\alpha.$ Existence is proved. ✓
Proof of Uniqueness. Suppose $a> 0$ and $b>0$ both satisfy $a^2=\alpha$ and $b^2=\alpha.$ Then: $$a^2 - b^2 = \alpha - \alpha = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; (a-b)(a+b) = 0.$$ Since $a+b> 0,$ $(a-b) = 0,$ so $a = b.$
Hence the non-negative square root is unique. ✓ $\blacksquare$
Notation and Convention
The unique non-negative real number $a$ satisfying $a^2=\alpha$ is written $\sqrt{\alpha}$ or $\alpha^{1/2}.$

Note: the theorem provides a non-negative square root. The equation $x^2=\alpha$ has two solutions $x=\pm\sqrt{\alpha}$ for $\alpha>0,$ but $\sqrt{\alpha}$ denotes only the non-negative one by convention.

Summary

The Full Proof at a Glance
Step What we did Tool used
1 Stated the theorem (existence & uniqueness of $\sqrt{\alpha}$) —
2 Handled the case $\alpha=0$: $a=0$ works and is unique Definition of product
3 For $\alpha>0$: defined $E_\alpha=\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x^2\le\alpha\}$ Set construction
4 $E_\alpha$ is nonempty: $0\in E_\alpha$; also $\tfrac{1}{n}\in E_\alpha$ for large $n$ Archimedean Property
5 $E_\alpha$ is bounded above by $N>\alpha$; applied LUB Property to get $a=\text{lub}(E_\alpha)>0$ Archimedean Property + LUB Property
6 Ruled out $a^2<\alpha$: found $b=a+\tfrac{1}{n}\in E_\alpha$, contradicting $a$ being an upper bound Archimedean Property, algebra: $\tfrac{1}{n^2}\le\tfrac{1}{n}$
7 Ruled out $a^2>\alpha$: found $c=a-\tfrac{1}{n}$ an upper bound $<a$, contradicting $a=\text{lub}$ Archimedean Property, algebra: $\tfrac{1}{n^2}\ge 0$
8 Concluded $a^2=\alpha$ by Trichotomy; proved uniqueness via $(a-b)(a+b)=0$ Law of Trichotomy, factoring
The Logical Chain
$$\underbrace{\text{LUB Property}}_{\text{Completeness of }\mathbb{R}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\text{Archimedean Property}}_{\mathbb{N}\text{ unbounded}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\sqrt{\alpha}\text{ exists in }\mathbb{R}}_{\text{this theorem}}$$ This theorem would fail in $\mathbb{Q}$ (e.g. $\sqrt{2}\notin\mathbb{Q}$) because $\mathbb{Q}$ does not have the LUB Property.
Key Technique: The Approximation Argument
The heart of Steps 6 and 7 is the same: to show $a = \text{lub}(E_\alpha)$ satisfies $a^2=\alpha,$ we did not compute $a$ explicitly. Instead we used a perturbation argument:
  • If $a^2<\alpha$: nudge $a$ up by $\tfrac{1}{n}$ to get a contradiction.
  • If $a^2>\alpha$: nudge $a$ down by $\tfrac{1}{n}$ to get a contradiction.
The Archimedean Property ensures the nudge $\tfrac{1}{n}$ can be made small enough in each case. Dropping the quadratic term $\tfrac{1}{n^2}$ (either by bounding it above by $\tfrac{1}{n},$ or below by $0$) simplifies the algebra.
Generalisations
The same method (define a set, take its LUB, show the LUB has the required property) proves:
  • $n$-th roots: Every $\alpha\ge 0$ has a unique non-negative $n$-th root $\alpha^{1/n}$ for any $n\in\mathbb{N}.$ Use $E_\alpha^{(n)} = \{x\ge 0\mid x^n\le\alpha\}$ and the same LUB argument.
  • Intermediate Value Theorem (special case): If $f$ is continuous and $f(a)<0<f(b),$ the set $\{x\in[a,b]\mid f(x)\le 0\}$ is nonempty and bounded above; its LUB is a zero of $f.$
  • Monotone Convergence Theorem: A bounded monotone increasing sequence converges; its limit is the LUB of the set of its terms.
Self-check Questions

Q1.  Where in the proof did we use $a > 0$?

Answer
In Step 7, we needed $c = a - \tfrac{1}{n} > 0$ (to be a valid upper bound candidate), which required choosing $n > \tfrac{1}{a}.$ Since $a>0,$ $\tfrac{1}{a}$ is finite and the Archimedean Property applies. Also in Step 6, the bound $a+\tfrac{1}{n}$ being the right candidate implicitly uses $a>0$ to ensure the contradiction is sharp.

Q2.  Why do we need both "$a^2<\alpha$ impossible" and "$a^2>\alpha$ impossible"?

Answer
The Law of Trichotomy says exactly one of the three relations holds. To conclude $a^2=\alpha$ we must eliminate the other two. Ruling out only one leaves the other as a possibility.

Q3.  Why is the proof that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational not a contradiction to this theorem?

Answer
The theorem says $\sqrt{2}\in\mathbb{R}$ exists. The proof that $\sqrt{2}\notin\mathbb{Q}$ says it is not rational. There is no contradiction: $\mathbb{Q}\subsetneq\mathbb{R},$ and irrational reals are perfectly valid real numbers.

Q4.  Modify the proof to show that every $\alpha\ge 0$ has a unique non-negative cube root.

Outline
Define $E_\alpha = \{x\ge 0\mid x^3\le\alpha\}.$ Show nonempty (contains $0$ and $\tfrac{1}{n}$ for large $n$), bounded above (by $N>\alpha$ since $N^3\ge N>\alpha$). Set $a=\text{lub}(E_\alpha).$
If $a^3<\alpha$: try $b=a+\tfrac{1}{n}$; expand $b^3$ and bound $\tfrac{1}{n^2},\tfrac{1}{n^3}$ by $\tfrac{1}{n}$ to get $b^3<\alpha.$
If $a^3>\alpha$: try $c=a-\tfrac{1}{n}$; bound $c^3$ from below. Same Archimedean argument closes both cases.
Q2: Find lub and glb of $\{a_n \mid a_1=1, a_{n+1}=3-\frac{1}{a_n}\}$
1. Monotonicity: The first few terms are $1, 2, 2.5, 2.6, \dots$ We can show the sequence is strictly increasing. Assume $a_n > a_{n-1}.$ Then: $$ a_{n+1} - a_n = \left(3 - \frac{1}{a_n}\right) - \left(3 - \frac{1}{a_{n-1}}\right) = \frac{1}{a_{n-1}} - \frac{1}{a_n} = \frac{a_n - a_{n-1}}{a_n a_{n-1}} > 0 $$
2. Convergence and Limit: Since it is increasing, let $L = \lim a_n$. Then: $$ L = 3 - \frac{1}{L} \implies L^2 - 3L + 1 = 0 $$ $$ L = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2} $$ Since terms are $> 1$, we take the positive root $L = \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 2.618$.
3. lub and glb: Since the sequence is increasing, the lub is the limit. The glb is the first term $a_1 = 1$. $$ \text{lub} = \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}, \quad \text{glb} = 1 $$
Q4: Find lub and glb of $\{a_n \mid a_1=\sqrt{2}, a_{n+1}=\sqrt{2+a_n}\}$
1. Monotonicity and Boundedness: We claim the sequence is increasing and bounded above by $2$.
Proof by Induction: Base: $a_1 = \sqrt{2} < 2$. Assume $a_n < 2$. Then $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2+a_n} < \sqrt{2+2} = 2$. Also, $a_{n+1}^2 - a_n^2 = (2+a_n) - a_n^2 = -(a_n^2 - a_n - 2) = -(a_n-2)(a_n+1)$. Since $a_n < 2$, this term is positive, so $a_{n+1} > a_n$.
2. Limit: Let $L = \lim a_n$. Then $L = \sqrt{2+L} \implies L^2 - L - 2 = 0$. $$ (L-2)(L+1) = 0 \implies L = 2 $$
3. lub and glb: The sequence is increasing, so the glb is the first term $a_1 = \sqrt{2}$. The sequence is bounded above and converges to $2$, so the lub is the limit $2$. $$ \text{lub} = 2, \quad \text{glb} = \sqrt{2} $$
Q5: Find lub and glb of $\{a_n \mid a_0=1, a_1=1+1/2, a_2=1+1/2+1/4, \dots\}$
1. Definition of the Sequence: The sequence represents the partial sums of a geometric series with ratio $r = 1/2$ and first term $1$. $$ a_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{2^k} = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2^2} + \dots + \frac{1}{2^n} $$
2. Closed Form Formula: Using the geometric series sum formula $S_n = a \frac{1-r^{n+1}}{1-r}$: $$ a_n = 1 \cdot \frac{1 - (1/2)^{n+1}}{1 - 1/2} = 2 \left( 1 - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}} \right) = 2 - \frac{1}{2^n} $$
3. Monotonicity and Bounds: Since each term $\frac{1}{2^n}$ is positive, $a_{n+1} = a_n + \frac{1}{2^{n+1}} > a_n$. The sequence is strictly increasing. From the formula $2 - \frac{1}{2^n}$, we can see that $a_n < 2$ for all $n$, and $a_n > 0$.
4. Limit: $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( 2 - \frac{1}{2^n} \right) = 2 $$
5. lub and glb: Since the sequence is increasing and converges to 2, the least upper bound is 2. The smallest value in the set is the first term $a_0 = 1$. $$ \text{lub} = 2, \quad \text{glb} = 1 $$
Q6: Find lub and glb of $\{a_n \mid a_1=1, a_2=1, a_n = \text{Area of regular polygon inscribed in unit circle}\}$
1. Geometric Analysis: Consider a circle of radius $R=1$. A regular polygon with $n$ sides (for $n \ge 3$) can be divided into $n$ isosceles triangles, each with a vertex angle of $\theta = \frac{2\pi}{n}$ and two sides of length 1.
The area of one such triangle is $\frac{1}{2} R^2 \sin(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right)$.
Therefore, the total area is: $$ a_n = n \cdot \frac{1}{2} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right) = \frac{n}{2} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right) \quad (\text{for } n \ge 3) $$
2. Behavior of the Sequence:
  • Given $a_1 = 1$ and $a_2 = 1$ (these are likely defined as base cases or placeholders, as $n$-gons require $n \ge 3$).
  • For $n \ge 3$, as $n$ increases, the polygon "fills" the circle more completely. The sequence is strictly increasing for $n \ge 3$.

3. The Limit (Archimedes' Method): Using the limit $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$, let $x = \frac{2\pi}{n}$. As $n \to \infty$, $x \to 0$. $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{2} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{n}\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \pi \cdot \frac{\sin(2\pi/n)}{2\pi/n} = \pi \cdot 1 = \pi $$
4. Visual Illustration:
As $n$ increases, the shaded polygon area approaches the area of the circle ($\pi$).

5. lub and glb: The sequence is bounded above by the area of the circle, $\pi$. Since it is increasing (for $n \ge 3$) and converges to $\pi$, the LUB is $\pi$. Based on the given definition $a_1=1, a_2=1$ and $a_3 \approx 1.3$, the minimum value in the set is 1. $$ \text{lub} = \pi, \quad \text{glb} = 1 $$
