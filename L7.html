Lecture 7 — Sequences

Definitions, examples, bounded sequences, convergence, visualisation, uniqueness of limits, and self-assessment.

1. Overview & Motivation

Why Sequences?
  • Analysis is built on the notion of a limit, which can be defined rigorously in terms of sequences.
  • Elementary functions — trigonometric, exponential, logarithm — can be approximated by sequences. With computers, such approximations can be made as accurate as needed.
  • A sequence is a special class of function: its domain is $\mathbb{N}$ and its range is a subset of $\mathbb{R}.$

In this lecture we introduce the formal definition of a sequence, study what it means for a sequence to converge, visualise sequences on the real line and in the plane, and prove that limits are unique.

2. Definition of a Sequence

Definition — Infinite Sequence
An infinite sequence of real numbers is a function $$f:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{R}.$$ We write $a_n = f(n)$ and denote the sequence by $(a_n),\; (a_n)_{n=1}^{\infty},$ or simply $a_1,a_2,a_3,\ldots$

Each $a_n$ is called the $n$-th term and $n$ is its index.
Notation. A sequence is an ordered list. We may also start the index at any integer $n_0$ (not just $1$), giving a function $f:\{n\in\mathbb{Z}:n\ge n_0\}\to\mathbb{R}.$

Order matters: $1,2,3,4,\ldots$ and $2,1,3,4,\ldots$ are different sequences.
Sequence vs Set
  • A set is a collection of distinct elements — no ordering, no repetition.
  • A sequence has a definite order and may repeat values.
Example: $(1,-1,1,-1,1,-1,\ldots)$ is a sequence whose terms come from the set $\{-1,1\}.$

Two ways to describe a sequence
(a) By a formula: $a_n = \dfrac{1}{n}$ defines every term at once.
(b) By listing: $\left(\tfrac{1}{1},\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{3},\ldots\right).$
We may also write $\left(\dfrac{1}{n}\right)_{n=1}^{\infty}.$ Starting the index at $n_0\ne 1$ can give simpler formulas (e.g. $b_n=2n$ with $n\ge 6$ gives $12,14,16,\ldots$).

4. Bounded Sequences

Definition — Bounded Sequence
A sequence $(a_n)$ is bounded if its range set $\{a_n:n\in\mathbb{N}\}$ is a bounded subset of $\mathbb{R}.$

Equivalently, $(a_n)$ is bounded if there exists $B\in\mathbb{R}$ such that $$|a_n|\le B \qquad\text{for all }n\in\mathbb{N}.$$
SequenceBounded?Reason

Note: A sequence can be bounded yet not converge: $\bigl((-1)^n\bigr)$ is bounded ($|a_n|=1$) but oscillates and never settles. Boundedness is necessary but not sufficient for convergence.

5. Convergence and Divergence

Definition — Convergence
The sequence $(a_n)$ converges to the number $L\in\mathbb{R},$ written $a_n\to L$ or $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=L,$ if $$\forall\,\varepsilon>0,\;\exists\,N\in\mathbb{N}:\; n>N\;\Longrightarrow\;|a_n-L|<\varepsilon.$$ Equivalently: $L-\varepsilon<a_n<L+\varepsilon$ for all $n>N.$

If no such $L$ exists, the sequence diverges.
Alternative form ($n\ge N$ vs $n>N$)
Some textbooks write $n\ge N$ instead of $n>N.$ Both are equivalent: the convergence or divergence of a sequence does not depend on any finite initial segment of terms.
— Using $n>N$: concerned with terms from index $N+1$ onwards.
— Using $n\ge N$: concerned with terms from index $N$ onwards.
Common Mistake — Incomplete Statement
Writing "$L-\varepsilon<a_n<L+\varepsilon$" alone is incomplete. You must also state: given $\varepsilon>0,$ you have found a positive integer $N$ such that the inequality holds for all $n>N.$ The existence of $N$ for every $\varepsilon$ is the heart of the definition.
The shortest maths joke: Let $\varepsilon<0$
Applying the definition of convergence to $\varepsilon$ gives index $N_1.$ Applying it to $\varepsilon/99$ gives a (possibly larger) index $N_2.$
Caution: $N_2$ is not necessarily $99\,N_1$ — it could be anything. The only guarantee is $N_2$ is some natural number that works for the smaller tolerance $\varepsilon/99.$

6. Visualising Sequences

There are two natural ways to display a sequence graphically.

On the Real Line
Plot each term $a_n$ as a point on the number line. Convergence is visible: the points cluster around the limit $L.$
In the Plane
Plot the points $(n,a_n)$ in the $xy$-plane. The horizontal axis is the index $n$; the vertical axis is the value $a_n.$ Convergence appears as the dots approaching a horizontal asymptote $y=L.$
$(1/n)$ visualised on the real line (dots cluster at $0$) and in the plane (dots approach the horizontal asymptote $y=0$).
$((-1)^{n+1})$ oscillates between $+1$ and $-1$ — it never converges to any $L.$

7. The $\varepsilon$-Band Picture

The definition of convergence becomes transparent in the plane. Given $\varepsilon>0,$ draw the horizontal band $(L-\varepsilon,\,L+\varepsilon).$ The definition says:

Geometric reading of convergence
$a_n\to L$ means: for every $\varepsilon>0,$ there exists $N$ such that all points $(n,a_n)$ with $n>N$ lie inside the $\varepsilon$-band $L-\varepsilon<y<L+\varepsilon.$

No matter how thin the band, eventually all dots are inside it.
The green dots (all $n>N$) lie inside the $\varepsilon$-band around $L.$ Red dots (early terms) may be anywhere.
The constant sequence. If $a_n=c$ for all $n,$ then for any $\varepsilon>0$ and any $N\ge 1$: $$|a_n-c|=|c-c|=0<\varepsilon.$$ So every constant sequence converges to its constant value, and any $N$ works.

8. Uniqueness of Limits

Theorem — Uniqueness of Limits
Every convergent sequence has a unique limit. That is, if $a_n\to L_1$ and $a_n\to L_2,$ then $L_1=L_2.$
Key idea. We show $|L_1-L_2|<\varepsilon$ for every $\varepsilon>0.$ Since a non-negative number that is smaller than every positive number must be $0,$ this forces $|L_1-L_2|=0,$ i.e., $L_1=L_2.$

Why does $|L_1-L_2|<\varepsilon$ for all $\varepsilon>0$ imply $L_1=L_2$? Suppose $L_1\ne L_2.$ Set $\varepsilon=\tfrac{|L_1-L_2|}{2}>0.$ Then $|L_1-L_2|<\varepsilon=\tfrac{|L_1-L_2|}{2}$ gives $|L_1-L_2|<\tfrac12|L_1-L_2|,$ which is impossible for a positive quantity. Contradiction.
Proof of uniqueness. Let $\varepsilon>0.$

Since $a_n\to L_1,$ there exists $N_1$ such that $n>N_1\Rightarrow|a_n-L_1|<\varepsilon/2.$
Since $a_n\to L_2,$ there exists $N_2$ such that $n>N_2\Rightarrow|a_n-L_2|<\varepsilon/2.$

Choose any $n>\max(N_1,N_2).$ By the Triangle Inequality: $$|L_1-L_2|=|L_1-a_n+a_n-L_2| \le|a_n-L_1|+|a_n-L_2| <\frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}=\varepsilon.$$ Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary, $|L_1-L_2|=0,$ so $L_1=L_2.$ $\blacksquare$
Why $\varepsilon/2$?
We need the sum of two small errors to be small. Using $\varepsilon/2$ for each guarantees their sum is $\varepsilon.$ This "$\varepsilon/2$-trick" (or "$\varepsilon/k$-trick" in general) appears throughout analysis.

9. First Worked Example: $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0$

Proposition
$\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}=0.$
Proof. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ By the Archimedean Property of $\mathbb{R},$ there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ with $N>\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}.$

For any $n>N$: $$\left|\frac{1}{n}-0\right|=\frac{1}{n}\le\frac{1}{N}<\varepsilon.$$ Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary, $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Second example: $\dfrac{n}{n+1}\to 1$
$\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1}-1\right|=\dfrac{1}{n+1}<\dfrac{1}{n}.$ Given $\varepsilon>0,$ choose $N>1/\varepsilon.$ For $n>N$: $\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1}-1\right|<\dfrac{1}{n}<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$
10: Prove $a_n = (1 - \frac{1}{2})(1 - \frac{1}{3})\dots(1 - \frac{1}{n+1})$ converges.
1. Simplify the Sequence Definition: The sequence is a product of $n$ terms. Let's rewrite the general term $k$ (where $k$ ranges from $2$ to $n+1$): $$ 1 - \frac{1}{k} = \frac{k-1}{k} $$ So, the sequence can be written as: $$ a_n = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{4} \cdot \dots \cdot \frac{n}{n+1} $$

2. Visualizing the Cancellation: This is a telescoping product. Notice how the numerator of one term cancels with the denominator of the previous term.
3. Closed Form Formula: Due to the massive cancellation, only the first numerator ($1$) and the last denominator ($n+1$) remain. $$ a_n = \frac{1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot \dots \cdot n}{2 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \cdot \dots \cdot (n+1)} = \frac{1}{n+1} $$

4. Convergence Proof: We evaluate the limit of the closed form expression: $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n+1} $$ As $n$ becomes arbitrarily large, the denominator $n+1$ tends to $\infty$, so the fraction tends to $0$. $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0 $$ Since the limit exists as a finite real number, the sequence converges (to 0).

10. Self-Assessment

Q1. Write down the first five terms of the sequence $a_n=\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n}.$ Is this sequence bounded? Does it converge?

Answer. Terms: $-1,\;\tfrac{1}{2},\;-\tfrac{1}{3},\;\tfrac{1}{4},\;-\tfrac{1}{5}.$
Bounded: $|a_n|=1/n\le 1$ for all $n.$ ✓
Convergence: $|a_n-0|=1/n\to 0,$ so $a_n\to 0.$ Use Step 3 of L8 (or direct $\varepsilon$-$N$): choose $N>1/\varepsilon.$

Q2. Prove from the definition that $\dfrac{2n+1}{n+1}\to 2.$

Proof. $\left|\dfrac{2n+1}{n+1}-2\right|=\left|\dfrac{2n+1-2(n+1)}{n+1}\right| =\dfrac{1}{n+1}<\dfrac{1}{n}.$ Given $\varepsilon>0,$ choose $N>1/\varepsilon.$ For $n>N$: the expression $<1/n<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q3. Show that $(-1)^n$ diverges using the definition of convergence.

Proof. Suppose $(-1)^n\to L.$ Choose $\varepsilon=\tfrac{1}{2}.$ There exists $N$ with $|(-1)^n-L|<\tfrac{1}{2}$ for all $n>N.$ For consecutive indices $n,n+1$ both $>N$: $$2=|(-1)^{n+1}-(-1)^n|\le|(-1)^{n+1}-L|+|(-1)^n-L|<\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{2}=1.$$ But $2<1$ is a contradiction. Hence $(-1)^n$ diverges. $\blacksquare$

Q4. Suppose $a_n\to L.$ Prove that $(a_n)$ is bounded.

Proof. Take $\varepsilon=1.$ There exists $N$ with $|a_n-L|<1$ for all $n>N.$ Then $|a_n|=|a_n-L+L|\le|a_n-L|+|L|<1+|L|$ for $n>N.$
Set $M=\max(|a_1|,\ldots,|a_N|,\,1+|L|).$ Then $|a_n|\le M$ for all $n.$ $\blacksquare$

Q5. If $a_n\to L$ and $a_n\to M,$ prove $L=M$ directly (re-derive uniqueness without using the $\varepsilon/2$-trick — try $\varepsilon=|L-M|/3$).

Proof. Suppose $L\ne M.$ Set $\varepsilon=|L-M|/3>0.$ Choose $N_1,N_2$ so the definition holds for each. For $n>\max(N_1,N_2)$: $|L-M|\le|L-a_n|+|a_n-M|<\varepsilon+\varepsilon=\tfrac{2}{3}|L-M|.$ But $|L-M|<\tfrac{2}{3}|L-M|$ is impossible. So $L=M.$ $\blacksquare$

Q6. True or False: "The convergence of $(a_n)$ depends on the first $100$ terms." Justify.

Answer: False. The definition requires the existence of $N$ beyond which $|a_n-L|<\varepsilon.$ We can always choose $N\ge 100,$ so the first $100$ terms are irrelevant. Changing any finite number of terms of a convergent sequence does not affect convergence or the limit.

Sequences & the $\varepsilon$-$N$ Definition — Connecting to Part C

The following questions are the core bridge between the abstract definition of convergence and working with absolute values.

Q13. Using the $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition, prove $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$

Proof. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ By the Archimedean property, choose $N\in\mathbb{N}$ with $N>1/\varepsilon.$ For $n\ge N$: $\left|\tfrac{1}{n}-0\right|=\tfrac{1}{n}\le\tfrac{1}{N}<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q14. Suppose $x_n\to x.$ Prove $|x_n|\to|x|.$

Proof. By the Reverse Triangle Inequality: $\bigl||x_n|-|x|\bigr|\le|x_n-x|.$ Given $\varepsilon>0,$ since $x_n\to x,$ there exists $N$ with $|x_n-x|<\varepsilon$ for all $n\ge N.$ Then $\bigl||x_n|-|x|\bigr|<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q15. Give an example of a divergent sequence $(x_n)$ such that $|x_n|$ converges. What does this show?

Answer. $x_n=(-1)^n$ diverges (oscillates between $+1$ and $-1$), but $|x_n|=1$ for all $n,$ so $|x_n|\to 1.$

This shows $x_n\to x\Rightarrow|x_n|\to|x|$ does not reverse in general. However, $|x_n|\to 0\iff x_n\to 0$ (the special zero-limit case where the converse holds).

Q16. Suppose $(x_n)$ is bounded ($|x_n|\le M$) and $y_n\to 0.$ Prove $x_n y_n\to 0.$

Proof. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ Since $y_n\to 0,$ choose $N$ with $|y_n|<\varepsilon/M$ for all $n\ge N.$ Then $|x_n y_n|=|x_n||y_n|\le M\cdot\tfrac{\varepsilon}{M}=\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q17. Prove: if $|x_n-x|<\dfrac{1}{n}$ for all $n\ge 1,$ then $x_n\to x.$

Proof. Given $\varepsilon>0,$ choose $N>1/\varepsilon$ (Archimedean). For $n\ge N$: $|x_n-x|<\tfrac{1}{n}\le\tfrac{1}{N}<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$
