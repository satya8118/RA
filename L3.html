Part I — Set Operations on Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$

Throughout this lecture let $A$ and $B$ be nonempty subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ and $c\in\mathbb{R}.$ We define three fundamental operations.

1. The Sumset $A+B$

Definition — Sumset
$$A+B \;=\; \{\,a+b \;\mid\; a\in A,\; b\in B\,\}$$ $A+B$ is the set of all possible sums formed by choosing one element from $A$ and one from $B.$

Examples with Finite Sets

$A$$B$$A+B$Remark
$\{1,2\}$$\{3,4\}$$\{4,5,6\}$ $|A+B|$ can be less than $|A|\cdot|B|$
$\{0,1\}$$\{-1,0,1\}$$\{-1,0,1,2\}$ Collisions: $0+1=1+0=1$
$\{1,3\}$$\{2,4\}$$\{3,5,7\}$ $1+4=3+2=5,$ only 3 distinct elements
Example with Intervals
$A=[0,1],\; B=[2,3]\;\Longrightarrow\; A+B=[2,4].$
In general, $[a_1,a_2]+[b_1,b_2]=[a_1+b_1,\;a_2+b_2].$

Questions — Think Before Clicking!

Q1.  Find a nonempty $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ with $A+A=A.$

Hint
Try a set large enough to absorb every sum it produces.
Solution
Two examples:
  • $A=\{0\}$: $\;A+A=\{0+0\}=\{0\}=A.$ ✓
  • $A=(-\infty,0]$: every sum $a_1+a_2\le 0,$ and every $x\le 0$ equals $x+0\in A+A,$ so $A+A=(-\infty,0]=A.$ ✓

Q2.  Find a nonempty $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ with $A+A\subsetneq A$ (a proper subset).

Hint
Try $A=\mathbb{N}=\{1,2,3,\ldots\}.$ What is the smallest possible sum?
Solution
$A=\{1,2,3,\ldots\}$: $A+A=\{n+m\mid n,m\ge 1\}=\{2,3,4,\ldots\}.$ Since $1\in A$ but $1\notin A+A,$ we get $A+A\subsetneq A.$ ✓

Q3.  Find a nonempty $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ with $A\subsetneq A+A.$

Hint
Try $A=\{0,1\}$ and compute $A+A.$
Solution
$A=\{0,1\}$: $A+A=\{0,1,2\}.$ Since $2\in A+A$ but $2\notin A$, we get $A\subsetneq A+A.$ ✓
Interval version: $A=(0,1)$ gives $A+A=(0,2)\supsetneq A.$ ✓

Q4.  Can $A+B=\emptyset$?

Solution
No. Since $A$ and $B$ are both nonempty there exist $a\in A$ and $b\in B,$ so $a+b\in A+B.$ Hence $A+B$ is always nonempty.

2. The Translation $c+A$

Definition — Translation
$$c+A \;=\; \{\,c+a \;\mid\; a\in A\,\}$$ Geometrically this is a rigid shift of $A$ by $c$ units along the number line.
Observation: $c+A$ is the special case of $A+B$ with $B=\{c\},$ i.e., $c+A=A+\{c\}.$

Definition — Scalar Multiple
$$cA \;=\; \{\,ca \;\mid\; a\in A\,\}$$ Geometrically this stretches or reflects $A$ about the origin.

Key contrast with $c+A$: $c+A$ is a rigid shift (no distortion). $cA$ stretches ($|c|>1$), compresses ($0<|c|<1$), or reflects ($c<0$) about the origin. Setting $c=0$ collapses everything to $\{0\}.$

Part II — The Least Upper Bound

1. Upper and Lower Bounds — A Quick Recall

Recall — Bounded Above / Bounded Below
A nonempty set $S\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ is:
  • Bounded above if $\exists\,M\in\mathbb{R}$ with $s\le M$ for all $s\in S.$ Any such $M$ is an upper bound of $S.$
  • Bounded below if $\exists\,m\in\mathbb{R}$ with $m\le s$ for all $s\in S.$ Any such $m$ is a lower bound of $S.$
Upper and lower bounds are never unique: if $M$ is an upper bound, so is $M+1,\;M+100,\;\ldots$

2. Definition of the Least Upper Bound

Among all the upper bounds of $S$ we single out the smallest one.

Definition — Least Upper Bound (LUB)
A number $L$ is called the least upper bound of $S$ if:
  1. $L$ is an upper bound of $S:$  $s\le L$ for all $s\in S.$
  2. $L$ is the smallest upper bound:  if $M$ is any upper bound of $S,$ then $L\le M.$
Definition — Greatest Lower Bound (GLB)
A number $l$ is called the greatest lower bound of $S$ if:
  1. $l$ is a lower bound of $S:$  $l\le s$ for all $s\in S.$
  2. $l$ is the largest lower bound:  if $m$ is any lower bound of $S,$ then $m\le l.$

3. Alternate Definition of LUB — Contrapositive Form

Condition (2) of the original definition says: every upper bound of $S$ is $\ge L.$ Taking the contrapositive: any number strictly less than $L$ is not an upper bound of $S.$ This leads to an equivalent — and often more useful — characterisation.

Alternate Definition — LUB (Contrapositive Form)
$L$ is the least upper bound of $S$ if and only if:
  1. $L$ is an upper bound of $S.$
  2. For every $\epsilon >0,$ there exists $s\in S$ such that $\;s>L-\epsilon.$
Condition (2) says: no number strictly smaller than $L$ can serve as an upper bound of $S.$ Equivalently, elements of $S$ can get arbitrarily close to $L$ from below.
Why are the two definitions equivalent? Original $\Rightarrow$ Alternate. Assume $L$ is the least upper bound. Let $\epsilon > 0.$ Then $L-\epsilon < L,$ so $L-\epsilon$ is not an upper bound. (since $L$ is the smallest upper bound). Hence there exists $s\in S$ with $s>L-\epsilon.$

Alternate $\Rightarrow$ Original. Suppose (1) and (2) hold. Let $M$ be any upper bound of $S.$ We want $L\le M.$ Suppose for contradiction $M < L.$ Set $\epsilon=L-M >0.$ By (2), there exists $s\in S$ with $s>L-\epsilon=M,$ contradicting the fact that $M$ is an upper bound. Hence $L\le M,$ so $L$ is the least upper bound. $\blacksquare$
Intuition: The alternate form captures the idea that the least upper bound has no gap between itself and the set: however small an $\varepsilon$ you choose, some element of $S$ lies within $\varepsilon$ of $L.$

4. The LUB is Unique — Notation $\text{lub}(A)$

Theorem — Uniqueness of the Least Upper Bound
A set $S$ can have at most one least upper bound. That is, if $L_1$ and $L_2$ are both least upper bounds of $S,$ then $L_1=L_2.$
Proof. Since $L_1$ is the least upper bound and $L_2$ is an upper bound, condition (2) gives $L_1\le L_2.$
Since $L_2$ is the least upper bound and $L_1$ is an upper bound, condition (2) gives $L_2\le L_1.$
Together, $L_1=L_2.$ $\blacksquare$
Because the least upper bound is unique when it exists, we write $$\text{lub}(S) \qquad\text{or}\qquad \text{lub}\,S$$ for the least upper bound of $S.$ Similarly, $\text{glb}(S)$ denotes the greatest lower bound.

These are well-defined objects — there is no ambiguity in the notation.

5. Maximum as a Least Upper Bound

Proposition — $\max(S)=\text{lub}(S)$ when the maximum exists
If $S$ has a maximum element $M^*$ (meaning $M^*\in S$ and $s\le M^*$ for all $s\in S$), then $M^*$ is the least upper bound of $S$: $$\max(S)\;\text{exists}\;\Longrightarrow\;\text{lub}(S)=\max(S).$$
Proof. (i) $M^*$ is an upper bound of $S$ by definition of maximum.
(ii) Let $M$ be any upper bound of $S.$ Since $M^*\in S,$ we have $M^*\le M.$

Both conditions are satisfied, so $\text{lub}(S)=M^*=\max(S).$ $\blacksquare$

Examples where $\max(S)$ exists

6. The LUB Need Not Belong to the Set

Unlike a maximum, $\text{lub}(S)$ does not have to be an element of $S.$

Key Insight
$\text{lub}(S)\in S \;\Longleftrightarrow\; S\text{ has a maximum and }\text{lub}(S)=\max(S).$

When $\text{lub}(S)\notin S,$ the set has no maximum — but the least upper bound still exists in $\mathbb{R}$ (guaranteed by the Completeness Axiom, coming shortly).

7. Using the LUB to Describe All Upper Bounds

Knowing $\text{lub}(S)$ lets us immediately write down the complete set of all upper bounds.

Proposition — Complete Set of Upper Bounds
If $S$ is bounded above, then $$\bigl\{\,M\in\mathbb{R}\;\mid\; M\text{ is an upper bound of }S\,\bigr\} \;=\; [\,\text{lub}(S),\,\infty).$$ In words: the upper bounds of $S$ are precisely all real numbers greater than or equal to $\text{lub}(S).$
Proof. Let $L=\text{lub}(S).$

Every $M\ge L$ is an upper bound. For any $s\in S$: $s\le L\le M.$ So $M$ is an upper bound. Hence $[L,\infty)\subseteq\{\text{upper bounds of }S\}.$

Every upper bound $M$ satisfies $M\ge L.$ Since $L$ is the least upper bound, $L\le M.$ Hence $\{\text{upper bounds of }S\}\subseteq[L,\infty).$

The two inclusions give equality. $\blacksquare$

Analogously for lower bounds: If $S$ is bounded below, then the set of all lower bounds of $S$ equals $(-\infty,\,\text{glb}(S)].$

Part III — Boundedness Results for Set Operations

We now assume $A$ and $B$ are bounded above and show that several natural operations preserve this property. We also compute the least upper bound of each resulting set.

Proposition 1 — Union
If $A$ and $B$ are bounded above, then $A\cup B$ is bounded above and $$\text{lub}(A\cup B) \;=\; \max\!\bigl(\text{lub}(A),\;\text{lub}(B)\bigr).$$
Proof — bounded above. Let $M_1=\text{lub}(A)$ and $M_2=\text{lub}(B).$ Set $M=\max(M_1,M_2).$ For any $x\in A\cup B$: if $x\in A$ then $x\le M_1\le M$; if $x\in B$ then $x\le M_2\le M.$ In either case $x\le M,$ so $M$ is an upper bound for $A\cup B.$
Let $N$ be an upper bound for $A\cup B.$ Then $M_1\le N$ and $M_2\le N.$ Hence $M\le N.$ $\blacksquare$
Why $\text{lub}(A\cup B)=\max(\text{lub}(A),\text{lub}(B))$?
Let $L_1=\text{lub}(A),\;L_2=\text{lub}(B),\;L=\max(L_1,L_2).$ Suppose WLOG $L=L_1.$ (i) $L$ is an upper bound (shown above). (ii) Any upper bound $M$ of $A\cup B$ is in particular an upper bound of $A,$ so $M\ge L_1=L.$ Hence $L=\text{lub}(A\cup B).$
Proposition 2 — Intersection (assuming $A\cap B\neq\emptyset$)
If $A$ and $B$ are bounded above and $A\cap B\neq\emptyset,$ then $A\cap B$ is bounded above and $$\text{lub}(A\cap B) \;\le\; \min\!\bigl(\text{lub}(A),\;\text{lub}(B)\bigr).$$
Proof — bounded above. Let $M_1=\text{lub}(A).$ Since $A\cap B\subseteq A,$ every $x\in A\cap B$ satisfies $x\le M_1.$ Hence $M_1$ is an upper bound for $A\cap B.$ (The same argument using $\text{lub}(B)$ works equally well.) $\blacksquare$
The inequality can be strict
$A=[0,2],\;B=[0.5,\,5]$: $A\cap B=[0.5,2],$ so $\text{lub}(A\cap B)=2.$ Here $\min(\text{lub}(A),\text{lub}(B))=\min(2,5)=2$ — equality.

But $\text{lub}(A\cap B)=2<5=\text{lub}(B),$ showing the inequality is strict on the $B$-side. In general $\text{lub}(A\cap B)\le\min(\text{lub}(A),\text{lub}(B)),$ and either equality or strict inequality is possible.
Proposition 3 — Sumset $A+B$
If $A$ and $B$ are bounded above, then $A+B$ is bounded above and $$\text{lub}(A+B) \;=\; \text{lub}(A)+\text{lub}(B).$$
Proof — bounded above. Let $M_1=\text{lub}(A)$ and $M_2=\text{lub}(B).$ Set $M=M_1+M_2.$ For any $x\in A+B$ write $x=a+b$ with $a\in A,\;b\in B.$ Then $x=a+b\le M_1+M_2=M.$ So $M$ is an upper bound for $A+B.$ $\blacksquare$
Why $\text{lub}(A+B)=\text{lub}(A)+\text{lub}(B)$? Let $L_1=\text{lub}(A),\;L_2=\text{lub}(B),\;L=L_1+L_2.$ We have shown $L$ is an upper bound for $A+B.$ It remains to show it is the least upper bound.

Let $\varepsilon>0.$ By the alternate definition of least upper bound: there exists $a\in A$ with $a>L_1-\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2},$ and there exists $b\in B$ with $b>L_2-\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}.$ Then $a+b\in A+B$ and $$a+b \;>\; \Bigl(L_1-\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\Bigr)+\Bigl(L_2-\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\Bigr) = L-\varepsilon.$$ Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary, $L$ is the least upper bound of $A+B.$

We look for an alternating proof.

It is clear that $L_1+L_2$ is an upper bound of $A+B.$
Let $M$ be an upper bound of $A+B.$
Let us fix $b\in B.$ Then $\forall a\in A\; a+b\le M.$ That is $a\le M-b.$
This shows that $M-b$ is an upperbound for $A.$ But $L_1$ is least upperbound for $A.$ Hence $L_1\le M-b.$
Equivalently $b\le M-L_1.$ Since $b$ is arbitrary, $M-L_1$ is upper bound for $B.$ Hence $L_1+L_2\le M.$ $\blacksquare$
Example: $A=[0,1],\;B=[-1,2].$ $\text{lub}(A)=1,\;\text{lub}(B)=2,$ so $\text{lub}(A+B)=3.$ Indeed $A+B=[-1,3],$ confirming $\text{lub}(A+B)=3.$ ✓
Proposition 4 — Translation $c+A$
If $A$ is bounded above, then $c+A$ is bounded above for every $c\in\mathbb{R},$ and $$\text{lub}(c+A) \;=\; c+\text{lub}(A).$$
Proof. Let $M=\text{lub}(A).$ For any $x=c+a\in c+A$ (with $a\in A$): $x=c+a\le c+M.$ So $c+M$ is an upper bound for $c+A.$

Minimality. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ By the alternate definition, there exists $a\in A$ with $a>M-\varepsilon,$ so $c+a\in c+A$ and $c+a>c+M-\varepsilon.$ Hence $c+M$ is the least upper bound of $c+A.$ $\blacksquare$
Consistency with Proposition 3: Since $c+A=A+\{c\}$ and $\text{lub}(\{c\})=c,$ Proposition 3 gives $\text{lub}(c+A)=\text{lub}(A)+c,$ confirming the formula.

Example: $A=(0,1),\;c=-3.$ $\text{lub}(A)=1,$ so $\text{lub}(c+A)=-3+1=-2.$ Indeed $c+A=(-3,-2),$ so $\text{lub}(c+A)=-2.$ ✓
Proposition 5 — Scaling $cA$ with $c>0$
If $A$ is bounded above and $c>0,$ then $cA$ is bounded above and $$\text{lub}(cA) \;=\; c\cdot\text{lub}(A).$$
Proof. Let $M=\text{lub}(A).$ For any $x=ca\in cA$ (with $a\in A$): since $c>0,$ $x=ca\le cM.$ So $cM$ is an upper bound for $cA.$

Minimality. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ There exists $a\in A$ with $a>M-\tfrac{\varepsilon}{c},$ so $ca\in cA$ and $ca>cM-\varepsilon.$ Hence $cM$ is the least upper bound of $cA.$ $\blacksquare$
⚠ What if $c<0$?
Multiplying by $c<0$ reverses the inequality: $a\le M\;\Rightarrow\;ca\ge cM.$ So $cA$ is bounded below, not above, and $$\text{glb}(cA) \;=\; c\cdot\text{lub}(A)\quad(c<0).$$ Example: $A=[0,1],\;c=-2.$ $\text{lub}(A)=1,$ so $\text{glb}(cA)=(-2)(1)=-2.$ Indeed $cA=[-2,0],$ confirming $\text{glb}(cA)=-2.$ ✓
