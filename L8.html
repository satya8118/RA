Real Sequences — Convergence & Key Theorems

A real sequence is a function $f:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{R}.$ We write its values as $x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots$ or $(x_n)_{n\ge 1}.$ The central question of this lecture is: does the sequence approach a fixed real number as $n\to\infty$?

Step 1 & What Does Convergence Mean? The Sequence $\left(\dfrac{1}{n}\right)$

Definition — Convergence of a Sequence
A sequence $(x_n)$ converges to $x\in\mathbb{R}$, written $x_n\to x$ or $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}x_n = x$, if: $$\forall\,\varepsilon>0,\quad\exists\,N\in\mathbb{N}\text{ such that } n\ge N \;\Longrightarrow\; |x_n - x| < \varepsilon.$$ If no such $x$ exists, the sequence diverges.
Unpacking the definition. $\varepsilon$ is a tolerance — how close we demand $x_n$ to be to $x.$ $N$ is the starting index from which the sequence stays within that tolerance. Crucially, $N$ may depend on $\varepsilon$: smaller $\varepsilon$ may require larger $N.$

Warm-up: $(x_n) = \left(\tfrac{1}{n}\right)$ converges to $0$

Claim: $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$
Reason: We need $\left|\dfrac{1}{n}-0\right| = \dfrac{1}{n} < \varepsilon,$ which holds whenever $n > \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}.$ So choose any integer $N > \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}$ (exists by the Archimedean Property).

Formal Proof: $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0$
Let $\varepsilon>0.$ By the Archimedean Property, there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ with $N > \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}.$ Then for all $n\ge N$: $$\left|\frac{1}{n} - 0\right| = \frac{1}{n} \;\le\; \frac{1}{N} \;<\; \varepsilon.$$ Since $\varepsilon>0$ was arbitrary, $\dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$ $\quad\blacksquare$
Remark. The choice of $N$ need not be the smallest valid index. Any $N$ satisfying $N > 1/\varepsilon$ works. Once one valid $N$ is found, all larger integers also work — the definition only requires existence.

Questions

Q1.  Using the definition, prove $\dfrac{n}{n+1}\to 1.$

Hint
Write $\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1}-1\right| = \dfrac{1}{n+1}.$ When is $\dfrac{1}{n+1}<\varepsilon$?
Solution
$\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1}-1\right| = \dfrac{1}{n+1} < \varepsilon$ $\Leftrightarrow n+1 > \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}$ $\Leftrightarrow n > \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}-1.$ Choose $N\in\mathbb{N}$ with $N>\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}-1.$ Then for $n\ge N$: $\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1}-1\right| = \dfrac{1}{n+1}\le\dfrac{1}{N+1}<\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q2.  Does $(-1)^n$ converge?

Solution
No. Suppose $(-1)^n\to L$ for some $L.$ Choose $\varepsilon=\tfrac{1}{2}.$ Then for all large $n$: $|(-1)^n - L|<\tfrac{1}{2}.$ But $|(-1)^{n+1}-(-1)^n|=2,$ yet by the triangle inequality $2\le|(-1)^{n+1}-L|+|(-1)^n-L|<1$ — contradiction. So $(-1)^n$ diverges.

Step 2 $x_n\to x$ $\Rightarrow$ $|x_n|\to|x|$, but Not Conversely

Proposition
If $(x_n)$ converges to $x,$ then $(|x_n|)$ converges to $|x|.$ $$x_n \to x \;\Longrightarrow\; |x_n| \to |x|.$$ The converse is false in general.
Proof ($\Rightarrow$). The key tool is the reverse triangle inequality: $$\bigl||x_n| - |x|\bigr| \;\le\; |x_n - x|.$$ Let $\varepsilon>0.$ Since $x_n\to x,$ there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n\ge N$: $|x_n-x|<\varepsilon.$ Then: $$\bigl||x_n|-|x|\bigr| \;\le\; |x_n-x| \;<\; \varepsilon.$$ Hence $|x_n|\to|x|.$ $\blacksquare$
The Converse is False — Counterexample
Let $x_n = (-1)^n.$ We showed $x_n$ diverges.

But $|x_n| = |(-1)^n| = 1$ for all $n,$ so $(|x_n|)$ is the constant sequence $1,$ which converges to $1$.

So $|x_n|\to 1$ yet $x_n$ does not converge. The converse fails.
Why does the converse fail?
The absolute value "forgets" the sign of $x_n.$ Even if $|x_n|$ settles down, the values $x_n$ themselves may keep alternating between $+|x_n|$ and $-|x_n|,$ never settling on a single limit.

A second example: $x_n = (-1)^n\left(1+\dfrac{1}{n}\right).$ Then $|x_n| = 1+\dfrac{1}{n}\to 1,$ but $x_n$ oscillates between values near $+1$ and $-1,$ so $x_n$ diverges.
When does $|x_n|\to|x|$ imply $x_n\to x$?
The implication $|x_n|\to|x|\;\Rightarrow\;x_n\to x$ holds in the special case $x=0.$ This is Step 3.

More generally, if we additionally know $x_n$ does not change sign infinitely often (e.g. $x_n\ge 0$ for all large $n$), then $|x_n|\to|x|$ with $x\ge 0$ does imply $x_n\to x.$

Step 3 $x_n\to 0$ $\Longleftrightarrow$ $|x_n|\to 0$

Theorem
For a real sequence $(x_n)$: $$x_n\to 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; |x_n|\to 0.$$

This is the one case where the converse of Step 2 does hold. The key observation is that $|x_n - 0| = |x_n| = ||x_n| - 0|,$ so the two convergence conditions are literally the same inequality.

Proof. Note that for any real number $y,$ $|y-0|=|y|.$ Therefore: $$|x_n - 0| = |x_n| \qquad\text{and}\qquad ||x_n| - 0| = |x_n|.$$ The condition "$|x_n-0|<\varepsilon$" and the condition "$||x_n|-0|<\varepsilon$" are both equivalent to "$|x_n|<\varepsilon$." Hence the two convergence statements are identical. $\blacksquare$
Why does the equivalence hold only at zero?
At a general limit $x\ne 0,$ the condition $|x_n-x|<\varepsilon$ involves the signed difference $x_n - x,$ while $||x_n|-|x||<\varepsilon$ involves the absolute values. The two are not the same unless $x=0.$

Useful applications of Step 3

Q.  Show $\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n}\to 0.$

Solution
$\left|\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n}\right| = \dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$ By Step 3, $\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n}\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Q.  Show $\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\to 0.$

Solution
$\left|\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\right| \le \dfrac{1}{n}\to 0.$ By the Squeeze / Sandwich Theorem and Step 3, $\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Step 4 — $(x_n)$ Converges, $(y_n)$ Bounded: What About $(x_n y_n)$?

Setup
Suppose $(x_n)$ converges to $x$ and $(y_n)$ is bounded (i.e. $|y_n|\le M$ for all $n$ and some $M>0$).

Question: Does $(x_n y_n)$ necessarily converge?

Case A: $x = 0$ — Yes, always converges

Theorem — $x_n\to 0$ and $(y_n)$ bounded $\Rightarrow$ $x_n y_n\to 0$
If $x_n\to 0$ and $|y_n|\le M$ for all $n,$ then $x_n y_n\to 0.$
Proof. Let $\varepsilon>0.$ Since $x_n\to 0,$ there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ such that $n\ge N\Rightarrow |x_n|<\dfrac{\varepsilon}{M}.$ Then for $n\ge N$: $$|x_n y_n - 0| = |x_n||y_n| \;\le\; |x_n|\cdot M \;<\; \frac{\varepsilon}{M}\cdot M = \varepsilon.$$ Hence $x_n y_n\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Case B: $x \ne 0$ — Not necessarily converges

Counterexample — $x\ne 0$, $(y_n)$ bounded, $(x_n y_n)$ diverges
Let $x_n = 2$ (constant, so $x_n\to 2$) and $y_n = (-1)^n.$
$(y_n)$ is bounded: $|y_n|=1\le 1.$ But $x_n y_n = 2(-1)^n,$ which diverges (oscillates between $+2$ and $-2$).

So $x_n\to 2\ne 0$ and $(y_n)$ bounded does not guarantee $(x_n y_n)$ converges.

Case C: $x \ne 0$ and $(y_n)$ converges — Yes, product converges

Theorem — Product Rule for Limits
If $x_n\to x$ and $y_n\to y,$ then $x_n y_n\to xy.$
Proof. Write: $$|x_n y_n - xy| = |x_n y_n - x_n y + x_n y - xy| \le |x_n||y_n - y| + |y||x_n - x|.$$ Since $(x_n)$ converges it is bounded: $|x_n|\le M$ for all $n.$ Let $\varepsilon>0.$ Choose $N$ so that for $n\ge N$: $|x_n-x|<\dfrac{\varepsilon}{2(|y|+1)}$ and $|y_n-y|<\dfrac{\varepsilon}{2M}.$ Then $|x_n y_n - xy| < M\cdot\dfrac{\varepsilon}{2M}+(|y|+1)\cdot\dfrac{\varepsilon}{2(|y|+1)} = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{2}+\dfrac{\varepsilon}{2}=\varepsilon.$ $\blacksquare$

Q.  Suppose $x_n\to 0$ and $y_n=\sin(n^2).$ Does $x_n y_n$ converge?

Solution
Yes. $|\sin(n^2)|\le 1$ for all $n,$ so $(y_n)$ is bounded. Since $x_n\to 0,$ by Case A, $x_n\sin(n^2)\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Q.  Let $x_n = 1 + \dfrac{1}{n}$ and $y_n = n.$ Is $(y_n)$ bounded? Does $(x_n y_n)$ converge?

Solution
$(y_n)=n$ is not bounded. Here $x_n y_n = n + 1\to\infty,$ so the product diverges. This example lies outside our theorem's hypotheses — boundedness of $(y_n)$ is essential.

Step 5 $\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n}\to 0$

Proposition
$\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\bigl(\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}\bigr) = 0.$

The terms $\sqrt{n+1}$ and $\sqrt{n}$ both grow to $\infty,$ yet their difference shrinks to zero. The trick is to rationalise.

Proof. Multiply and divide by the conjugate: $$\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n} = \frac{(\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n})(\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n})}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}} = \frac{(n+1)-n}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}}.$$ Since $\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}\ge\sqrt{n}+\sqrt{n}=2\sqrt{n},$ we have: $$0 \;\le\; \sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}} \;\le\; \frac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}.$$ Since $\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}\to 0,$ by the Squeeze Theorem, $\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}\to 0.$ $\blacksquare$

Q.  Does $\sqrt{n^2+n}-n$ converge? If so, find the limit.

Hint
Rationalise: $\sqrt{n^2+n}-n = \dfrac{n^2+n-n^2}{\sqrt{n^2+n}+n} = \dfrac{n}{\sqrt{n^2+n}+n}.$ Divide numerator and denominator by $n.$
Solution
$\sqrt{n^2+n}-n = \dfrac{n}{\sqrt{n^2+n}+n} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1+1/n}+1}\to\dfrac{1}{1+1}=\dfrac{1}{2}.$ So $\sqrt{n^2+n}-n\to\dfrac{1}{2}.$ $\blacksquare$
Q15: Prove $a_n = \frac{3}{10} + \frac{3}{100} + \dots + \frac{3}{10^n}$ converges to $\frac{1}{3}$.
1. Rewrite using Summation Notation: The sequence is the sum of the first $n$ terms of a geometric series where the first term is $\frac{3}{10}$ and the ratio is $\frac{1}{10}$: $$ a_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{3}{10^k} = \frac{3}{10} + \frac{3}{100} + \frac{3}{1000} + \dots $$

2. Derive the Closed Form Formula: Factor out $3$ from each term: $$ a_n = 3 \sum_{k=1}^n \left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^k $$ Using the sum formula for a finite geometric series $\sum_{k=1}^n ar^{k-1} = a \frac{1-r^n}{1-r}$: Let $a = \frac{3}{10}$ and $r = \frac{1}{10}.$ $$ \sum_{k=1}^n \left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^k = \frac{1}{10} \frac{1 - (1/10)^n}{1 - 1/10} = \frac{1}{10} \frac{1 - 10^{-n}}{9/10} = \frac{1}{9} (1 - 10^{-n}) $$ Multiply by the outer factor of 3: $$ a_n = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{9} (1 - 10^{-n}) = \frac{1}{3} (1 - \frac{1}{10^n}) $$

3. Evaluate the Limit: As $n \to \infty$, the term $10^n$ grows without bound, so $\frac{1}{10^n} \to 0.$ Therefore: $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{3} \left(1 - \frac{1}{10^n}\right) = \frac{1}{3} (1 - 0) = \frac{1}{3} $$
Prove that every convergent sequence is bounded.
Statement: If $(x_n)$ is a sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ and $\lim_{n \to \infty} x_n = L$ (where $L \in \mathbb{R}$), then $(x_n)$ is bounded. That is, there exists $M > 0$ such that $|x_n| \le M$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}.$

Proof:
  1. Since $x_n \to L$, by the definition of limit, for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$, we have $|x_n - L| < \epsilon.$
  2. Let us specifically choose $\epsilon = 1.$ Then there exists $N$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ |x_n - L| < 1 $$ By the reverse triangle inequality, this implies: $$ |x_n| - |L| \le |x_n - L| < 1 \implies |x_n| < |L| + 1 $$ This shows that all terms from index $N$ onward are bounded by $|L| + 1.$
  3. Now, consider the "finite troublemakers" — the first $N-1$ terms: $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{N-1}.$ Since this is a finite set of real numbers, it has a maximum absolute value. Let: $$ M_1 = \max \{ |x_1|, |x_2|, \dots, |x_{N-1}| \} $$
  4. Finally, define $M = \max \{ M_1, |L| + 1 \}.$
Then for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, we have $|x_n| \le M.$ Therefore, the sequence $(x_n)$ is bounded. $\blacksquare$
Is the converse true? (If bounded, then convergent?)
Claim: The converse is false. Boundedness does not imply convergence.

Counterexample 1 (Oscillating): Consider the sequence $x_n = (-1)^n.$
The set of values is $\{-1, 1\}$. Clearly, $|x_n| \le 1$ for all $n$, so the sequence is bounded.
However, the sequence alternates between $-1$ and $1$ forever. It does not approach any single real number. (Formally, for $L=1$, the odd terms are distance $2$ away; for $L=-1$, the even terms are distance $2$ away; for any other $L$, all terms are at least some positive distance away). Thus, $\lim_{n \to \infty} (-1)^n$ does not exist.

Counterexample 2 (Diverging to $\pm\infty$ is impossible if bounded, but what about multiple limits?): Let $x_n = 1$ if $n$ is prime, and $x_n = 0$ if $n$ is composite.
This sequence is bounded between $0$ and $1$. However, it has subsequences converging to $0$ and subsequences converging to $1$, so the overall limit does not exist.

Historical Note: While boundedness $\not\Rightarrow$ convergence, the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem states that every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence.
If $x_n \to x$ and $x > 0$, then eventually all terms are greater than $x/2.$
Statement: Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence such that $\lim_{n \to \infty} x_n = x$ with $x > 0.$ Prove that there exists an $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$, $x_n > \frac{x}{2}.$

Proof:
  1. Since $x > 0$, the number $\frac{x}{2}$ is strictly positive.
  2. Choose $\epsilon = \frac{x}{2} > 0$ in the definition of the limit.
  3. Since $x_n \to x$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ |x_n - x| < \frac{x}{2} $$ This double inequality can be written as: $$ -\frac{x}{2} < x_n - x < \frac{x}{2} $$ Adding $x$ to all parts: $$ x - \frac{x}{2} < x_n < x + \frac{x}{2} $$ $$ \frac{x}{2} < x_n < \frac{3x}{2} $$
Therefore, for all $n \ge N$, we have $x_n > \frac{x}{2} > 0.$ $\blacksquare$
Prove the analogous statement for a negative limit.
Statement: Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence such that $\lim_{n \to \infty} x_n = x$ with $x < 0.$ Prove that there exists an $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$, $x_n < \frac{x}{2}.$ (Note: since $x<0$, $x/2$ is also negative).

Proof:
  1. Since $x < 0$, its absolute value $|x| = -x$ is strictly positive.
  2. Choose $\epsilon = \frac{|x|}{2} = -\frac{x}{2} > 0$ in the definition of the limit.
  3. Since $x_n \to x$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ |x_n - x| < -\frac{x}{2} $$ Expanding the absolute value: $$ -\left(-\frac{x}{2}\right) < x_n - x < -\frac{x}{2} $$ $$ \frac{x}{2} < x_n - x < -\frac{x}{2} $$ Adding $x$ to all parts: $$ \frac{x}{2} + x < x_n < x - \frac{x}{2} $$ $$ \frac{3x}{2} < x_n < \frac{x}{2} $$
Therefore, for all $n \ge N$, we have $x_n < \frac{x}{2}.$ Since $x < 0$, it follows that $x_n < \frac{x}{2} < 0,$ meaning the terms are eventually strictly negative. $\blacksquare$
General "Sign Preservation" theorem.
Theorem (Sign Preservation / Limit away from zero): Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence such that $\lim_{n \to \infty} x_n = x,$ where $x \neq 0.$ Then there exists $\delta > 0$ and $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ |x_n| \ge \delta > 0 $$ Equivalently, the sequence $(x_n)$ is eventually bounded away from zero and maintains the same sign as $x.$

Proof: We divide this into two cases based on the sign of the non-zero limit $x.$
Case 1: $x > 0$
By Question 10, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ x_n > \frac{x}{2} $$ Since $x_n > 0$, this implies $|x_n| = x_n > \frac{x}{2}.$ Let $\delta = \frac{x}{2} > 0.$ Then $|x_n| \ge \delta$ for all $n \ge N.$
Case 2: $x < 0$
By Question 11, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ x_n < \frac{x}{2} < 0 $$ Since $x_n$ is negative, its absolute value is $|x_n| = -x_n.$ Multiplying the inequality $x_n < \frac{x}{2}$ by $-1$ (reversing the sign): $$ -x_n > -\frac{x}{2} \implies |x_n| > \frac{|x|}{2} $$ Let $\delta = \frac{|x|}{2} > 0.$ Then $|x_n| \ge \delta$ for all $n \ge N.$

Conclusion: In both cases, we found a strictly positive lower bound $\delta$ (specifically, $\delta = \frac{|x|}{2}$) such that eventually $|x_n| \ge \delta.$ This proves the sequence never approaches $0$ and permanently retains the sign of its limit. $\blacksquare$
Definition — Sequence is NOT Convergent
To say a sequence $(x_n)$ is not convergent, we negate the standard convergence definition for every possible real number $L$.

The sequence $(x_n)$ does not converge if for every $L \in \mathbb{R}$: $$ \exists \epsilon > 0 \text{ such that } \forall N \in \mathbb{N}, \; \exists n \ge N \text{ with } |x_n - L| \ge \epsilon $$
Translation: No matter which candidate limit $L$ you pick, there is a specific "error margin" $\epsilon$ such that you can never get the tail of the sequence to stay strictly within that margin of $L.$ There will always be "troublemaker" terms $x_n$ (arbitrarily far out in the sequence) that break the rule.
Example 1: Oscillating Sequence ($x_n = (-1)^n$)
Consider the sequence $x_n = (-1)^n = \{ -1, 1, -1, 1, \dots \}.$
Why it doesn't converge to $L = 1$:
Choose $\epsilon = 1.$ For any $N$ (no matter how large), there exist infinitely many odd integers $n > N.$ For these terms, $x_n = -1.$ The distance is: $$ |x_n - L| = |-1 - 1| = 2 \ge \epsilon $$ Since this happens infinitely often, the sequence cannot settle at $1.$
Why it doesn't converge to any $L \in (-1, 1)$: The terms only take the values $1$ and $-1$. If $L$ is strictly between them, say $L=0$, the distance to either $1$ or $-1$ is at least $1.$ Choose $\epsilon = 0.5.$ The terms $1$ and $-1$ will always violate $|x_n - 0| < 0.5.$
Example 2: Unbounded Sequence ($x_n = n$)
Consider the sequence $x_n = n = \{1, 2, 3, 4, \dots \}.$ This sequence goes to infinity.
Why it doesn't converge to any finite $L$:
Suppose we test a candidate limit $L.$ Let's choose $\epsilon = 1.$
By the Archimedean Property, we can find an $n$ such that $n > L + 1.$
The distance is: $$ |x_n - L| = |n - L| = n - L > 1 = \epsilon $$ Since such $n$ exist arbitrarily far out in the sequence, the tail never stays inside the interval $(L-1, L+1).$
Example 3: Bounded but not Convergent
Consider the sequence: $x_n = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } n \text{ is prime} \\ 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is composite or } n=1 \end{cases}$ Sequence: $0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1, \dots$
Why it doesn't converge to $L=1$: Choose $\epsilon = 0.4.$
By Euclid's Theorem, there are infinitely many composite numbers (and 1). For these indices, $x_n = 0.$ $$ |x_n - 1| = |0 - 1| = 1 \ge 0.4 $$ Since the sequence hits $0$ infinitely often (the "troublemaker" terms), it cannot get arbitrarily close to $1$ and stay there.

Why it doesn't converge to $L=0$: Similarly, there are infinitely many primes where $x_n = 1.$ Choose $\epsilon = 0.4.$ The distance to $0$ will be $\ge 1$ infinitely often.

This example shows that even if a sequence is bounded (all terms are either 0 or 1), it may not have a limit.
